# Danika — Phase One Review Questions

**Purpose:** Prepare a respectful and useful Phase Two conversation  
**For:** Danika Lewis  
**Status:** Questions for review — no answer is assumed  
**Version:** 1.0

## Before answering

These questions are not a test, an application form, or a request for immediate commitment.

They exist because the current Danika project is a proposal created from limited information. Your answers will help us separate what is true from what has been interpreted, and what is genuinely valuable from what only sounds interesting on paper.

You do not need to answer everything in writing. You may:

- answer only the questions that feel important;
- mark a question private;
- say “I do not know yet”;
- correct the premise of a question;
- discuss the questions in a live call;
- ask for any information to be removed or reframed.

## A simple first response

Before the detailed questions, five short reactions are enough to begin:

1. What part of the proposal feels most accurate?
2. What part feels least accurate?
3. What part feels most exciting?
4. What part makes you uncertain or uncomfortable?
5. Would you like to explore a second phase together?

## 1 — Your understanding of the proposal

1. In your own words, what do you believe the Danika project is proposing?
2. Does the presentation reflect an idea you recognise as connected to you?
3. Which parts reflect something you have actually wanted to create?
4. Which parts feel like Amir's interpretation rather than your intention?
5. Is there anything presented too confidently that should remain an open question?
6. Is there anything important about the original idea that the proposal has missed?

## 2 — Your work and expertise

7. How do you currently describe your professional work?
8. In which areas do people already seek your advice, guidance, or support?
9. What experience, training, qualifications, or lived knowledge do you consider central to your work?
10. Are there areas in which you do **not** want to be presented as an expert?
11. What problems do you feel especially capable of helping people navigate?
12. What kind of transformation can you responsibly help someone achieve?
13. Are there claims, outcomes, or forms of coaching that would feel misleading or ethically inappropriate?

## 3 — The people you may want to serve

14. Who are the people you most naturally want to help?
15. What situation are they usually in when they need support?
16. What have they already tried before reaching someone like you?
17. What prevents them from making progress?
18. Would you prefer to work with individuals, groups, organisations, or a combination?
19. Are there audiences or situations you would not want the product to serve?
20. Should the initial experience focus on one narrow use case? If so, which one?

## 4 — The experience you want to create

21. Should the first version be primarily a coaching service, a guided digital product, an AI-assisted experience, or a combination?
22. Which parts must involve you personally?
23. Which parts could be supported by a trained team member?
24. Which parts could responsibly be assisted by AI?
25. Which parts should never be delegated to AI?
26. What should a person understand, feel, or possess after completing the experience?
27. What would make the experience meaningfully different from a chatbot, online course, consultant, therapist, or traditional coach?
28. Would you prefer to begin with a manual service pilot before building software?

## 5 — Your method and intellectual property

29. Do you already have a repeatable way of helping people, even if it is not formally documented?
30. What questions do you tend to ask first?
31. What patterns or frameworks do you repeatedly use?
32. Do you have notes, exercises, articles, recordings, workshops, client examples, or other material that could help document your method?
33. Which parts of your knowledge should remain private?
34. Which parts could become public education, a product, a book, a course, or a trained AI system?
35. How should your original ideas and methods be credited and protected?

## 6 — AI, trust, and human control

36. What is your current experience with AI tools?
37. What would you want an AI collaborator to help you do?
38. What would you never want an AI system to infer, decide, retain, or publish?
39. Should users always know when they are interacting with AI?
40. What information would be too sensitive to store?
41. When should the system stop and refer a person to a human or qualified professional?
42. What would make you trust the system enough to use it with real people?
43. What would make you withdraw that trust?

## 7 — Identity, name, and public association

44. Are you comfortable with the working name “Danika” being used for this proposal?
45. Are you comfortable with your full name appearing publicly in the current repository or website?
46. If not, what name or description should be used instead?
47. Do you want to be described as an inspiration, subject-matter contributor, founder, partner, advisor, client, or none of these at this stage?
48. Which statements about you require correction or removal?
49. May the current work remain public while it is under review?
50. Are there images, biography details, conversations, examples, or personal stories that must not be used?

## 8 — Ownership and collaboration

51. Which parts of the original idea do you consider yours?
52. Which parts do you consider jointly explored or created?
53. What contribution would you be interested in making if the project continues?
54. How much time could you realistically dedicate during an exploration phase?
55. What decisions would require your explicit approval?
56. What type of collaboration would feel fair and sustainable?
57. Before further work, which ownership or compensation questions must be clarified in writing?
58. Who else, if anyone, should be included in these discussions?

## 9 — Business and practical reality

59. Would you want this to become a business, a professional tool, a public resource, a personal method, or something else?
60. Would you feel comfortable charging people for the experience? Under what conditions?
61. What could the smallest valuable paid offering be?
62. What evidence would you need before investing significant time or money?
63. Are there professional, legal, safeguarding, insurance, or regulatory issues we should investigate?
64. What resources, relationships, audience access, or existing assets could support a pilot?
65. What constraints could make the project unrealistic at this time?

## 10 — The first experiment

66. If we had to test only one promise with three to five people, what should it be?
67. Who could safely participate in a private pilot?
68. What should participants receive by the end?
69. What would count as a meaningful success?
70. What would tell us to stop or change direction?
71. How much direct involvement would you want in the pilot?
72. Should the pilot use your name publicly, privately, or not at all?

## 11 — Your decision after Phase One

73. Which direction best represents your current position?

- Continue exploring
- Continue, but revise the proposal first
- Keep the useful work but separate it from my name
- Pause and revisit later
- Stop the project in its current form
- I need more information before deciding

74. What must happen before you would feel comfortable continuing?
75. What would you like the next conversation to accomplish?
76. Is there anything you want documented now so it cannot be misunderstood later?

## Suggested next step

If the direction feels worth exploring, the next step is a **60–90 minute Phase Two Alignment Workshop**.

The workshop would not attempt to design the entire product. It would focus on five decisions:

1. What is true?
2. What must be corrected?
3. What should remain private?
4. What is the smallest valuable experiment?
5. What relationship, if any, should Danika have with the project?

## Response options

You may respond in whichever way feels easiest:

- brief written notes;
- comments against individual questions;
- a recorded voice response;
- a live review call;
- a private document;
- an initial “continue / revise / pause / stop” decision followed by details later.

## Closing note

The purpose of this review is not to defend the proposal.

The purpose is to make it more truthful.

Your corrections are not obstacles to the work; they are the evidence needed for the work to become real.

# نسخهٔ فارسی — پرسش‌های بازبینی دانیکا

این پرسش‌ها آزمون یا درخواست تعهد فوری نیستند. هدف آن‌ها این است که مشخص شود چه بخش‌هایی از پیشنهاد فعلی واقعی، چه بخش‌هایی برداشت و چه بخش‌هایی صرفاً پیشنهاد Lewis OS هستند.

لازم نیست دانیکا به همهٔ پرسش‌ها کتبی پاسخ دهد. می‌تواند پاسخ را خصوصی اعلام کند، بگوید هنوز نمی‌داند، پیش‌فرض سؤال را اصلاح کند یا پاسخ‌ها را در یک تماس زنده مطرح کند.

## پنج پاسخ اولیهٔ کافی برای شروع

1. کدام قسمت پیشنهاد از همه دقیق‌تر است؟
2. کدام قسمت کمترین تطابق را با واقعیت دارد؟
3. کدام قسمت هیجان‌انگیزتر است؟
4. کدام قسمت باعث تردید یا ناراحتی می‌شود؟
5. آیا این جهت‌گیری ارزش بررسی در فاز دوم را دارد؟

## موضوعات اصلی جلسهٔ بازبینی

- برداشت دانیکا از پیشنهاد و اصلاح سوءبرداشت‌ها
- تعریف دقیق تخصص، تجربه و مرزهای حرفه‌ای
- مخاطب و مسئله‌ای که واقعاً باید حل شود
- شکل تجربه: کوچینگ، سرویس، محصول دیجیتال یا ترکیبی
- متد و دارایی‌های فکری دانیکا
- نقش مجاز و غیرمجاز هوش مصنوعی
- حریم خصوصی، اعتماد و شرایط ارجاع به انسان
- اجازهٔ استفاده از نام و هویت عمومی دانیکا
- نقش احتمالی، مالکیت و مدل همکاری
- امکان تجاری، محدودیت‌ها و ریسک‌های حقوقی
- کوچک‌ترین پایلوت معنادار
- تصمیم ادامه، بازنویسی، جداسازی، توقف موقت یا پایان

## کال تو اکشن پیشنهادی

اگر جهت کلی ارزش بررسی دارد، قدم بعدی یک ورکشاپ هماهنگی ۶۰ تا ۹۰ دقیقه‌ای است که فقط پنج تصمیم را روشن می‌کند:

1. چه چیزهایی واقعیت دارند؟
2. چه چیزهایی باید اصلاح شوند؟
3. چه چیزهایی باید خصوصی بمانند؟
4. کوچک‌ترین آزمایش ارزشمند چیست؟
5. رابطهٔ دانیکا با پروژه، در صورت تمایل، چه باید باشد؟

هدف این بازبینی دفاع از پیشنهاد نیست؛ هدف واقعی‌تر کردن آن است.
