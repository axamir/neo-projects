# Governance

Version: 0.1

Status: Living Document

---

# Purpose

Governance defines how decisions, ownership, consent, publication, and responsibility are handled across Lewis OS projects.

The goal is to prevent ambiguity before it becomes conflict.

---

# Core Governance Principles

- Ownership must be explicit.
- Consent must be documented.
- Silence is not consent.
- Private material remains private by default.
- Public association requires approval.
- Important decisions must be recorded.
- Project history must remain traceable.
- AI may assist, but humans remain accountable.

---

# Roles

## Founder

The person who introduces, owns, or leads the original venture idea.

Responsibilities may include:

- defining intent
- reviewing key decisions
- approving public claims
- protecting project direction
- confirming ownership boundaries

## Architect

The person responsible for transforming the idea into a structured venture system.

Responsibilities may include:

- project architecture
- documentation
- workflow design
- risk identification
- roadmap development
- continuity planning

## Builder

The person or team responsible for implementation.

Responsibilities may include:

- technical execution
- design
- testing
- deployment
- documentation updates
- reporting blockers

## Advisor

A person who contributes specialist knowledge without automatically receiving ownership or authority.

## Investor

A person or entity providing capital under a separate written agreement.

## AI Systems

AI may assist with research, drafting, coding, review, analysis, and automation.

AI systems do not own project assets and do not replace human approval.

---

# Ownership

Ownership must be defined separately for:

- original idea
- project name
- brand
- source code
- documentation
- design assets
- business model
- datasets
- domain names
- repositories
- generated outputs
- jointly created work

No ownership transfer is assumed without written agreement.

---

# Consent

Explicit approval is required before:

- publishing a person's name
- presenting someone as a founder, partner, or advisor
- using private conversations
- publishing personal history
- sharing confidential material
- transferring repositories
- licensing project assets
- selling or assigning project rights

Consent may be revised or withdrawn subject to existing legal obligations.

---

# Privacy Classes

Project material should be classified as:

## Private

Visible only to approved participants.

## Internal

Available to the working team.

## Reviewable

Prepared for review but not public.

## Public

Approved for publication.

## Licensed

Approved for use under specific terms.

---

# Decision Records

Important decisions should include:

- decision
- date
- participants
- evidence considered
- alternatives
- approval status
- consequences
- next review date

Decisions should not exist only inside chat history.

---

# Versioning

All important documents should include:

- version number
- status
- last updated date
- source or owner
- approval state

Major changes should be traceable through Git history.

---

# Publication

A project may only be published when:

- ownership is clear
- sensitive material is removed
- public claims are reviewed
- names and roles are approved
- links and sources are verified
- the current status is accurately represented

---

# Repository Transfer

When a project graduates into an independent repository, the handover should include:

- approved source documents
- repository history or documented origin
- ownership statement
- contributor credits
- license
- implementation brief
- roadmap
- issue backlog
- access permissions
- privacy boundaries

The original incubation record may remain in Neo Projects as historical evidence.

---

# Conflict Resolution

Disagreements should be handled through:

1. written clarification
2. review of existing agreements
3. evidence-based discussion
4. documented proposed resolution
5. mediation if necessary
6. legal review where required

---

# Governance Rule

No project should scale faster than its ownership, consent, and responsibility structures can support.
